Reflection

This subject has really allowed me to have fun; experiment with new technology and also have the opportunity to write JavaScript in an application's setting. I really enjoyed the time I spent programming, and the time I spent solving the shere volume of problems that Trove threw at me.

The Design

We chose groups quite early on in the semester, which is always wise in my opinion. Early on I had my heart set on doing an AuzzieMon clone but unfortunately that idea got shot down in a hail of fire. So we changed tack and went with the timeline idea.

Which hat is mine?

Once we had an idea chosen; we were then tasked with 'wearing' each of the 6 coloured hats from Edward de Bono. I am really good at being a white and a black hat. I think it's because I approach a problem from a very logical, programming perspective.
Unfortunately I have great trouble seperating myself from the implementation phase of any project. During the design phase, I frequently shot down ideas(in my head) that I considered too hard to implement. I had this problem in DECO2500 as well where I would shoot down ideas(also internally) which were unfeasable to implement. I think it comes from my programming background; always focusing on if a problem is solveable, and not on the UX of that idea.

The Build

I was designated lead programmer and got stuck into making the application tick.

False Start

With JavaScript and CSS comming back into fashion as web browsers become more forward thinking and standards compliant(IE *shakes fist*) this project seemed perfect to use the newer compile-to languages like CoffeeScript and SCSS. So I set my heart on using them. Unfortunately I overlooked the fact that I wasn't working alone. This caused problems because my group would edit the compiled JavaScript, then I would edit the CoffeeScript and when it compiled, the JavaScript changes would be overwritten. This meant that I changed everything back to plain CSS and JavaScript.

A slightly better start

Unfortunately when I moved over to JavaScript, I kept the singleton class style that I had written in CoffeeScript(where it's clearer). This made the code really hard to read and then throw in JavaScript's scoping for even more debugging fun.
What I realise now is that having the singleton pattern is really bad practice in the circumstances that I used them. It made the code harder to understand, develop and debug.

Slowly does it

After finally managing to get the timetable to display; I finally started loading LOTS of data. Which lead to another problem. Speed. Every time new data needed to be added; lots of data changed, the massive DOM was rearanged frequently. This made the page simply freeze every time new data was added. Obviously this was unacceptable, so we solved it by limiting the amount of data loaded. A stop gap solution obviously.

Where Have You Been

And then Masonry found its way into my Google searches. It is the answer I had been looking for. This made all the code that I had written to organise the timeline redundant; but it made the timeline responsive(slightly usable on mobile), fast and look better.

The Reflection

Well this is a little bit meta, but here we go. This site was an absolute pain to put together at first. The most important question flowing through my mind was: How am I going to make it look decent? . I started out with my go-to design. Floating header, fixed footer, and grey; The more grey the better, Use all the greys. But that really didn't feel right to me. It felt too 'Bootstrappy'.

Material to the Rescue

So I decided I'd go with another option. After reading a couple of groups final reports for DECO3801, I noticed that they had used Google's Material Design; so I borrowed most of the guidelines. Single primary color; and other colors derrived from it. This really helped me get over the 'blank canvas syndrome' and get on with actually creating content for the site.

Language Choice

This was my own project; no-one else needed to look at it; so I decided to use SCSS. It has made life so easy. The number of times I've needed to change the padding on the footer; and it has just flowed through. Plus I think it makes the style sheets easier to read and manage

Overall

I really enjoyed the course. My group was fantastic, doing the reports and 'fluffy' stuff whilst I worked on the website. I came into this course treating it as a 'easy 7' subject; but I think the work level was sufficient.

Teaching

I didn't go to any of the lectures after the first few. The slides were consise and clear giving me the information I needed without having to sit through it. Lots of the lecture's content just didn't apply to me due to my experiences on projects both inside and outside university.
I really didn't find the workshops usefull at all. I guess this was because I am proficient in all the neccessary languages and I also wasn't doing any of the extra stuff around the website such as the poster.
I did however appreciate the contact times as a time to work on the project whilst also having the group there to bounce ideas off.

Blogging

I did like the requirement to write a blog. I tended to write really short posts where I just wanted to do the 'assigned' post; and really long essays when I was given free reign. I also found that I have a distinct preference to write very informally(also on this site). It's possibly my personality to use humor to make myself feel at ease.
